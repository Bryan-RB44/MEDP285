Mini-Intro

Even though we live under starless skies, I still spite our light pollution by looking at the night sky anyways. As most of our fellow New Yorkers (or anyone living in cities for that matter) don't have the opportunity to see what the sky holds after dark, I'll share whatever photos I have from my roughly two years of experience with using telescopes to show what lies hidden every night.

Deep Space Objects

Thingas

M51 - The Whirlpool Galaxy

The Whirlpool Galaxy is one of the few things to be seen year-round here. It's just barely bright enough to be seen visually but still a perfect target for photography! It alongside its companion (as it's actually two galaxies) make for a great target to start at when looking at objects outside our solar system!

Image Credit: Bryan Briones
Equipment & Software: Zwo Seestar S50, Adobe Lightroom

M20 and M21 - Webb's Cross and the Trifid Nebula

M21 and the Trifid Nebula (top left and bottom right respectively) are amazing sights to see during the Summer months. The former is a group of tightly packed stars while the latter are star-forming parts of our galaxy. It's a great sight to see both visually and with a camera with their brightness, with the benefit of seeing more objects as you look deep and closeby our galaxy's core which isn't too far away from this pair of objects.

Image Credit: Bryan Briones
Equipment & Software: Zwo Seestar S50, Adobe Lightroom

NGC 3628, M66, and M65 - "Leo's Triplet"

After seeing double twice, we now see a group of triplets! Leo's Triplet is a close group of galaxies that are bright enough to be seen in the Spring skies with ease. Without a doubt they're something to knock off any photographer's or astronomer's bucket list, especially when there's even more galaxies nearby them (which I haven't been able to capture in just one photo).

Image Credit: Bryan Briones
Equipment & Software: Zwo Seestar S50, Adobe Lightroom
