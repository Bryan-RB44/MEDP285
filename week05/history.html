History of the Color Lavender

The color lavender has a rich history dating back to ancient times. It was first used by the Egyptians in their art and in the process of mummification. The ancient Greeks also valued the color and used it to dye their clothing. In the 16th century, the color was used in the royal courts of Europe as a symbol of power and wealth.

During the Victorian era, lavender became associated with refinement and elegance, particularly in England. Women's clothing often featured lavender-colored fabrics, and the color was also used for interior design, particularly in wallpaper and drapery. The popularity of lavender continued throughout the 20th century, particularly in the 1960s and 1970s, when it was associated with the hippie movement and the counterculture.

Originally, the name lavender only applied to flowers. By 1930, the book A Dictionary of Color identified three major shades of lavender; lavender, lavender gray, and lavender blue, and in addition a fourth shade of lavender called old lavender (a darker lavender gray).

By 1955, the publication of the ISCC-NBS Dictionary of Color Names (a color dictionary used by stamp collectors to identify the colors of stamps), now on the Internet, listed dozens of different shades of lavender. Today, although the color floral lavender (the color of the flower of the lavender plant) remains the standard for lavender, just as there are many shades of pink (light red, light rose, and light magenta colors), there are many shades of lavender (some light magenta, some light purple, light violet [as well as some grayish-violet], and some light indigo colors).

Interesting Bonus Facts About Lavender

  1. The name for the color lavender actually came from the plant first! It shares this naming convention with the color orange and lime as well.
  2. Its name comes from the Latin word "lavare," which means "to wash," due to its traditional use in perfumes and soaps.
  3. The color plum actually count as both lavender and magenta!
  4. The color lavender actually represented the LGBTQ community, to the point which most members from the baby boomer generation still believe it's the "gayest" color.
  5. Lavender roses are considered symbols of "love at first sight".
